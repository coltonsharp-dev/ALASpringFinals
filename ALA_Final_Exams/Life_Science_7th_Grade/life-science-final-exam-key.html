Section A · Multiple Choice (1–65)
Question 1Non-Contact Forces
Which of the following is a non-contact force?
Solution
A non-contact force can act on an object without touching it. Friction, tension, and a push all require physical contact. Gravity pulls between any two objects with mass even across empty space — the only non-contact force in the list.
Question 2Non-Contact Forces
Two magnets are placed near each other and they push apart. This means:
Solution
Magnetic rule: like poles repel, opposite poles attract. If the magnets push apart, that's repulsion — so like poles are facing each other.
Question 3Non-Contact Forces
As the distance between two objects with mass increases, the gravitational force between them:
Solution
Gravity gets weaker as objects get farther apart. It never reaches zero (it just keeps shrinking) and gravity is never negative — it only pulls. So gravity decreases.
Question 4Non-Contact Forces
A balloon is rubbed on someone's hair, then sticks to a wall. The non-contact force responsible is:
Solution
Rubbing the balloon transfers electrons onto it, giving it a negative charge. The charged balloon induces an opposite charge in the wall, and opposite charges attract. This is static electricity.
Question 5Non-Contact Forces
When an object becomes negatively charged, what particles have moved onto it?
Solution
Electrons are the particles that move during charging — protons stay locked in the nucleus, and neutrons have no charge. Gaining electrons → object becomes negative.
Question 6Non-Contact Forces
Gravity on the Moon is about 1/6 of Earth's. An astronaut's mass on the Moon, compared to Earth, is:
Solution
Mass is the amount of matter in an object — it doesn't change with location. Weight (the pull of gravity) IS smaller on the Moon, but mass stays exactly the same.
Question 7Non-Contact Forces
The region of space around a magnet where it can exert a force on another magnet is called the:
Solution
Every non-contact force has a field — the area in which the force acts. For magnets, that's the magnetic field. (Gravity has a gravitational field; charged objects have an electric field.)
Question 8Non-Contact Forces
Which best describes weight?
Solution
Weight = mass × gravity. It's a force, measured in newtons. "Amount of matter" is mass; "space taken up" is volume. Weight is the gravitational pull on an object's mass.
Question 9Non-Contact Forces
Object A has a charge of +2. Object B has a charge of −3. The electric force between them will be:
Solution
Charge rule: like charges repel, opposite charges attract. One is positive (+2), one is negative (−3) — opposite signs — so the force is attractive.
Question 10Non-Contact Forces
If you snap a bar magnet exactly in half, what happens?
Solution
A magnet's poles come from the alignment of tiny magnetic regions inside it. No matter how many times you cut it, each piece becomes a new magnet with its own N and S pole. Magnets cannot have just one pole.
Question 11Cells
The basic unit of all living things is the:
Solution
From cell theory: the cell is the smallest unit that is alive. Atoms are non-living building blocks; tissues and organs are made up of many cells.
Question 12Cells
Which organelle is often called the "powerhouse of the cell" because it produces most of the cell's energy?
Solution
Mitochondria perform cellular respiration, breaking down glucose to release ATP (chemical energy). That's why they're nicknamed the powerhouse.
Question 13Cells
Which structure is found in plant cells but NOT in animal cells?
Solution
All four are in plant cells, but only the cell wall (and chloroplasts and the large central vacuole) are missing in animal cells. The cell wall gives plants rigid structure.
Question 14Cells
Photosynthesis takes place primarily in which organelle?
Solution
Chloroplasts contain chlorophyll, the green pigment that captures sunlight. They turn CO₂ + water + light → glucose + O₂. Only plants and some other organisms have them.
Question 15Cells
Which statement is NOT part of the cell theory?
Solution
A, B, and C are the three parts of cell theory. D is FALSE — prokaryotic cells (like bacteria) have DNA but NO membrane-bound nucleus, and they're still cells.
Question 16Cells
The "control center" of a eukaryotic cell, where DNA is stored, is the:
Solution
The nucleus contains the cell's DNA — the instructions that direct everything the cell does. It controls cell growth, division, and protein production.
Question 17Cells
Prokaryotic cells (such as bacteria) do NOT have which of the following?
Solution
Prokaryotes DO have DNA, a cell membrane, and cytoplasm — but their DNA floats freely in the cytoplasm. Only eukaryotes (plants, animals, fungi, protists) wrap their DNA in a membrane-bound nucleus.
Question 18Cells
The thin barrier that controls what enters and leaves a cell is the:
Solution
The cell membrane is "selectively permeable" — it lets some things in (food, oxygen) and keeps other things out, while sending wastes out. Plants have a cell membrane and a cell wall outside it.
Question 19Cells
Which list shows the levels of organization from smallest to largest?
Solution
Smallest to largest: cell → tissue → organ → organ system → organism. Many cells of the same kind form a tissue; tissues form organs; organs form systems.
Question 20Cells
Which equation best summarizes photosynthesis?
Solution
Plants take in CO₂ from the air and water from the soil, use sunlight as energy, and produce glucose (food) + oxygen. Choice A is the reverse — that's cellular respiration.
Question 21Cells
Cellular respiration releases usable energy by breaking down which molecule?
Solution
In the mitochondria: glucose + oxygen → CO₂ + H₂O + ATP. The chemical bonds in glucose store energy, and respiration unlocks that energy as ATP for the cell to use.
Question 22Human Body Systems
Which body system pumps blood through the body?
Solution
The circulatory system = heart + blood + blood vessels (arteries, veins, capillaries). Its job is to pump and distribute blood to every cell in the body.
Question 23Human Body Systems
The diaphragm is a major muscle for which body system?
Solution
The diaphragm is a dome-shaped muscle below the lungs. When it contracts (flattens), the chest expands and air rushes IN; when it relaxes, air pushes OUT. It powers breathing for the respiratory system.
Question 24Human Body Systems
Where does most chemical digestion and absorption of nutrients occur?
Solution
The stomach mainly mashes food and starts breaking down proteins. Most chemical digestion (with help from enzymes from the pancreas and liver) AND nutrient absorption happens in the small intestine, which is lined with tiny villi to maximize surface area.
Question 25Human Body Systems
Which system is made up of the brain, spinal cord, and nerves and sends signals throughout the body?
Solution
The nervous system uses fast electrical signals through neurons. The endocrine system also sends messages, but using slower chemical hormones, not nerves.
Question 26Human Body Systems
The main job of red blood cells is to carry:
Solution
Red blood cells contain hemoglobin, an iron-rich protein that grabs oxygen at the lungs and releases it at the body's tissues. (They also help carry some CO₂ back to the lungs.)
Question 27Human Body Systems
Bones meet other bones at structures called:
Solution
Joints are where bones meet (knees, elbows, knuckles). Tendons connect muscle to bone; ligaments connect bone to bone.
Question 28Human Body Systems
The kidneys, which filter wastes from the blood, are part of which system?
Solution
The excretory system (also called urinary system) includes kidneys, ureters, bladder, and urethra. Kidneys filter wastes — especially urea — out of the blood and form urine.
Question 29Human Body Systems
Hormones are chemical messengers released by which system?
Solution
The endocrine system includes glands like the pituitary, thyroid, and adrenal glands. They release hormones into the blood to slowly regulate growth, mood, energy, and more.
Question 30Human Body Systems
The respiratory and circulatory systems work together to:
Solution
In the lungs' alveoli, oxygen passes into the blood while CO₂ leaves it. The respiratory system handles air-to-blood exchange; the circulatory system carries those gases between lungs and cells. Together: gas exchange.
Question 31Human Body Systems
Skin, hair, and nails make up the:
Solution
The integumentary system is the body's outer covering. It protects against pathogens, regulates temperature (sweat), and senses touch.
Question 32Human Body Systems
Which is NOT a function of the skeletal system?
Solution
Bones DO support, protect, and produce blood cells (in red bone marrow). Pumping blood is the heart's job — that's the circulatory system, not skeletal.
Question 33Weather & Biomes
What is the difference between weather and climate?
Solution
Weather = what's happening RIGHT NOW or today (rain, heat, snow). Climate = the long-term pattern over decades. "It's snowing today" is weather. "Phoenix is hot and dry" is climate.
Question 34Weather & Biomes
A rapidly rotating column of air that touches the ground is a:
Solution
Tornadoes form during thunderstorms when warm and cold air masses collide and rotate. They're narrow, very fast, and short-lived — but the strongest can have wind speeds over 200 mph.
Question 35Weather & Biomes
Hurricanes form over:
Solution
Hurricanes need warm ocean water (about 80 °F or warmer) for fuel. Warm water evaporates quickly, releasing heat and moisture that powers the storm. That's why hurricanes form near the equator and weaken over land or cold water.
Question 36Weather & Biomes
A long period of below-average rainfall is called a:
Solution
A drought is a long stretch of unusually low precipitation. It can damage crops, dry up reservoirs, and worsen wildfires — serious problems in the Southwest.
Question 37Weather & Biomes
Which biome has the highest biodiversity (the most different species)?
Solution
Tropical rainforests have warm temperatures year-round, abundant rain, and lots of sunlight — the perfect conditions for an enormous variety of plants and animals to thrive.
Question 38Weather & Biomes
The biome characterized by permafrost (permanently frozen soil) and short summers is the:
Solution
The tundra is found near the Arctic Circle. Soil stays frozen year-round below the surface (permafrost), so trees can't take root. Plants are short and life is sparse.
Question 39Weather & Biomes
Cacti, kangaroo rats, and rattlesnakes are all adapted to which biome?
Solution
All three are classic desert species — very common right here around Vistancia! Cacti store water; kangaroo rats get water from their food; rattlesnakes hunt at night to avoid heat.
Question 40Weather & Biomes
The vast majority of Earth's water is found in the:
Solution
About 97% of Earth's water is salty ocean water. Glaciers/ice caps hold about 2%; only ~1% is the freshwater we can actually drink (rivers, lakes, groundwater).
Question 41Weather & Biomes
The amount of solar energy a region receives depends most on its:
Solution
Near the equator, sunlight hits Earth almost straight on, concentrating energy. Near the poles, the same sunlight spreads over more area at a low angle, so it's much weaker. Latitude is the biggest driver of climate.
Question 42Weather & Biomes
In the eye of a hurricane, air pressure is:
Solution
Hurricanes are intense low-pressure systems. The eye is the lowest-pressure part of the storm — that's why air rushes in toward it from all directions, fueling the spinning winds in the eyewall.
Question 43Weather & Biomes
Which biome typically has 4 distinct seasons and trees that lose their leaves each fall (deciduous)?
Solution
The temperate forest (much of the eastern U.S., Europe, parts of Asia) has cold winters, warm summers, and trees like oak and maple that drop their leaves to conserve water during the cold season.
Question 44Plate Tectonics
The thinnest, outermost layer of Earth (where we live) is the:
Solution
From the surface inward: crust → mantle → outer core → inner core. The crust is rocky and thin (5–70 km thick) compared to Earth's ~6,400 km radius.
Question 45Plate Tectonics
Which layer of Earth is a liquid made mostly of melted iron and nickel?
Solution
The outer core is liquid iron and nickel. The inner core is also iron/nickel but is solid because of extreme pressure. The motion of the liquid outer core generates Earth's magnetic field.
Question 46Plate Tectonics
The scientist who first proposed that the continents had drifted apart over time was:
Solution
Alfred Wegener (1912) noticed that continents like Africa and South America fit together like puzzle pieces and shared matching fossils and rocks. His idea was rejected at first because he couldn't explain HOW they moved — later confirmed by plate tectonics.
Question 47Plate Tectonics
The supercontinent that existed about 250 million years ago, before breaking apart, was:
Solution
Pangaea (Greek for "all lands") broke apart into Laurasia (north) and Gondwana (south), which kept splitting until they formed today's continents. Atlantis is mythological.
Question 48Plate Tectonics
Tectonic plates move because of:
Solution
Hot rock in the mantle rises, cools near the crust, sinks back down, and gets reheated — a cycle called a convection current. These slow currents drag the plates above them.
Question 49Plate Tectonics
At a divergent plate boundary, plates are:
Solution
Memory tip: "di-VERGE" = "go in different directions" → moving apart. Magma rises into the gap, cools, and creates new crust.
Question 50Plate Tectonics
A mid-ocean ridge forms at which type of plate boundary?
Solution
When two oceanic plates pull apart, magma rises and creates a long underwater mountain range — a mid-ocean ridge. The Mid-Atlantic Ridge is the most famous example. This is also where sea-floor spreading happens.
Question 51Plate Tectonics
The Himalayan mountains formed when two continental plates collided. This is what type of boundary?
Solution
When two continental plates collide, neither one can subduct (they're both light/thick). Instead the crust crumples upward into giant mountains. The Indian plate ramming into the Eurasian plate built the Himalayas, and they're still growing today.
Question 52Plate Tectonics
The San Andreas Fault in California is a famous example of what kind of plate boundary?
Solution
At a transform boundary, plates slide horizontally past each other. They get stuck, build pressure, and snap — producing earthquakes. The Pacific Plate slides past the North American Plate along the San Andreas Fault.
Question 53Plate Tectonics
When a denser oceanic plate meets a less dense continental plate, the oceanic plate:
Solution
Oceanic crust is made mostly of basalt and is denser than the granite-rich continental crust. So the oceanic plate subducts down into the mantle, often forming a deep ocean trench and a chain of volcanoes inland.
Question 54Plate Tectonics
The rigid outer shell of Earth (the crust + upper mantle), broken into tectonic plates, is called the:
Solution
"Litho-" means rock. The lithosphere is the solid rocky outer shell that's broken into the plates. Beneath it is the asthenosphere — hotter, partly soft mantle that the plates slide on.
Question 55Periodic Table
The atomic number of an element equals the number of:
Solution
Atomic number = number of protons. The number of protons defines the element. (Hydrogen = 1, helium = 2, lithium = 3, and so on.) In a neutral atom, the number of electrons matches.
Question 56Periodic Table
The mass number of an atom equals:
Solution
Almost all of an atom's mass is in the nucleus, which holds protons + neutrons. Electrons are about 2,000 times lighter, so they don't really count toward mass. Subtract atomic # from mass # to get neutrons.
Question 57Periodic Table
The horizontal rows on the periodic table are called:
Solution
Memory tip: "PERIOD = ROW" (think of a sentence ending — the period is at the end of a horizontal line). Periods tell you how many electron shells an element has.
Question 58Periodic Table
The vertical columns on the periodic table are called:
Solution
Groups (families) are the columns. Elements in the same group share similar chemical properties because they have the same number of valence (outer) electrons.
Question 59Periodic Table
The element with the symbol Na is:
Solution
Na = sodium (from Latin natrium). Other Latin-based symbols to know: K = potassium (kalium), Fe = iron (ferrum), Au = gold (aurum), Ag = silver (argentum).
Question 60Periodic Table
The noble gases (Group 18) are best described as:
Solution
Noble gases (helium, neon, argon, krypton, xenon, radon) have a "full" outer electron shell, so they don't need to react with other elements. Inert = doesn't easily react.
Question 61Periodic Table
Which group contains the most reactive metals?
Solution
Group 1 alkali metals (lithium, sodium, potassium…) have just one outer electron, which they "want" to lose. Sodium reacts violently with water; potassium is even worse. (Hydrogen sits in Group 1 but is not a metal.)
Question 62Periodic Table
Which subatomic particle has no electric charge?
Solution
Memory tip: NEUtron = NEUTRAL. Protons are +, electrons are −, neutrons have no charge (they balance the nucleus's mass without affecting charge).
Question 63Periodic Table
Which element is a metalloid (has properties of both metals and nonmetals)?
Solution
Metalloids sit on the staircase line dividing metals from nonmetals (B, Si, Ge, As, Sb, Te). Silicon is the most famous — it's a semiconductor used in computer chips. Iron and sodium are metals; oxygen is a nonmetal.
Question 64Periodic Table
An atom of carbon (C) has atomic number 6. How many protons does it have?
Solution
Atomic number = number of protons. Carbon's atomic number is 6, so it has 6 protons. (Most carbon also has 6 neutrons, giving it a mass number of 12.)
Question 65Periodic Table
Most of the elements on the periodic table are classified as:
Solution
Roughly 75% of the periodic table is metals — everything to the left of the staircase line. Metals are typically shiny, conductive, malleable (bendy), and ductile (can be stretched into wire).

Section C · Free Response (76–80)
Question 76FRQ · Cells
Compare and contrast a plant cell and an animal cell.

(a) List at least three organelles found in BOTH plant and animal cells, and briefly describe the job of each.

(b) List at least two structures found ONLY in plant cells and explain why a plant needs each one.

(c) Animal cells often look round or irregular while plant cells look more like rectangles or boxes. Explain why plant cells have this shape.
(a) Organelles in BOTH (any 3)
Nucleus — control center; stores DNA and directs the cell's activities.
Mitochondria — "powerhouse"; performs cellular respiration to release energy from glucose.
Ribosomes — build proteins by reading instructions from the nucleus.
Cell membrane — controls what enters and leaves the cell.
Cytoplasm — jelly-like fluid that holds the organelles and is where many reactions happen.
Endoplasmic reticulum / Golgi apparatus — package and ship proteins.
(b) ONLY in Plant Cells (any 2)
Cell wall — a rigid layer outside the cell membrane made of cellulose. It gives the plant structural support so the plant can stand up without bones.
Chloroplasts — contain chlorophyll and carry out photosynthesis. Plants need them because they make their own food using sunlight.
Large central vacuole — stores water, nutrients, and waste, and helps keep the plant cell firm by pushing outward against the cell wall.
(c) Why Plant Cells Are Box-Shaped
Plant cells have a rigid cell wall outside their cell membrane. The cell wall doesn't bend or shift much, so the cell holds a fixed, geometric box shape. Animal cells have only a flexible cell membrane (no wall), which is why they can be round, flat, or irregular and can change shape easily.
Question 77FRQ · Body Systems
Describe how the respiratory system and the circulatory system work TOGETHER to deliver oxygen from the air all the way to the cells in your body, and to remove carbon dioxide.

In your answer, you must use and correctly explain at least five of these terms: lungs, alveoli, diaphragm, red blood cells, hemoglobin, capillaries, heart, oxygen, carbon dioxide, mitochondria.
Model Response · Path of an Oxygen Molecule
Step 1. When the diaphragm contracts and flattens, the chest expands and air is pulled into the body. The air travels through the nose and trachea into the lungs.

Step 2. Inside the lungs, the air ends up in tiny sacs called alveoli. Each alveolus is wrapped in tiny blood vessels called capillaries, and the walls of both are very thin. Oxygen diffuses out of the alveolus and into the blood.

Step 3. Oxygen attaches to hemoglobin inside red blood cells. The blood (now oxygen-rich) flows back to the heart, which pumps it out through arteries to the rest of the body.

Step 4. At each body cell, the blood travels through capillaries again. Oxygen diffuses out of the red blood cells, into the cells, and into the mitochondria. There, the oxygen is used in cellular respiration to release energy from glucose.

Step 5 (return trip). The waste gas carbon dioxide from cellular respiration enters the blood, travels back through the heart to the lungs, diffuses into the alveoli, and is exhaled when the diaphragm relaxes.

Big idea: The respiratory system handles air ↔ blood exchange. The circulatory system handles blood ↔ cells transport. Neither system can deliver oxygen to a cell on its own — they only work as a team.
Question 78FRQ · Plate Tectonics
Compare the three main types of plate boundaries: convergent, divergent, and transform. For each, describe (a) what the plates are doing, (b) one geological feature or event that forms there, and (c) one real-world example.
Convergent Boundary
(a) Motion: Plates collide / push toward each other.
(b) Feature/event: Mountain ranges (continent–continent), volcanoes and ocean trenches (oceanic–continental subduction), island arcs (oceanic–oceanic). Earthquakes are common.
(c) Example: Himalayas (India + Eurasia colliding); Andes Mountains and Cascade Range volcanoes (oceanic plates subducting under continental plates).
Divergent Boundary
(a) Motion: Plates move apart from each other.
(b) Feature/event: Mid-ocean ridges (under the sea) where new crust forms; rift valleys (on land); shallow earthquakes.
(c) Example: Mid-Atlantic Ridge (between the Americas and Europe/Africa); the East African Rift Valley.
Transform Boundary
(a) Motion: Plates slide horizontally past each other in opposite directions.
(b) Feature/event: Faults and frequent earthquakes. (No volcanoes — crust is not being created or destroyed.)
(c) Example: San Andreas Fault in California, where the Pacific Plate slides past the North American Plate.
Question 79FRQ · Weather & Biomes
The tropical rainforest biome has very high biodiversity, while the desert biome has much lower biodiversity.

(a) Identify at least three abiotic factors that explain why the rainforest can support so many species.

(b) Identify at least two abiotic factors that make the desert harsh.

(c) Describe two adaptations — one rainforest, one desert — and explain how each helps the organism survive.
(a) Why the Rainforest Has High Biodiversity (any 3)
Warm, stable temperatures year-round — no harsh winter to survive, so cold-blooded animals and tropical plants thrive all year.
Heavy, consistent rainfall (often 80+ inches a year) — abundant water for plants and the animals that feed on them.
Plenty of sunlight near the equator — powers photosynthesis and supports a thick, layered canopy.
Nutrient-rich, layered habitat (forest floor, understory, canopy, emergent layer) — many different niches for different species to live in.
(b) Why the Desert Is Harsh (any 2)
Very low precipitation — less than 10 inches per year. Water is the limiting resource for life.
Extreme temperatures — very hot during the day, often very cold at night, which is hard on living things.
Intense sunlight and high evaporation — what little water exists evaporates quickly.
Poor, sandy soil — few nutrients for plants.
(c) Two Adaptations
Rainforest example — sloths: Sloths move very slowly and have algae-tinted fur, which camouflages them from predators in the green canopy. Their slow metabolism also lets them survive on a low-energy diet of leaves.

Desert example — cacti: Cacti have thick, waxy stems that store water and a small surface area (with spines instead of leaves) that reduces water loss. Their shallow, wide roots quickly soak up rain when it does fall. These adaptations let cacti survive months without rain.
Question 80FRQ · Periodic Table + Forces
An atom of magnesium (Mg) has an atomic number of 12 and a mass number of 24.

(a) How many protons, neutrons, and electrons does a neutral magnesium atom have?

(b) In which group and period of the periodic table is magnesium located?

(c) List two general physical properties of metals.

(d) Two magnesium ions (each +2) are placed near each other. What kind of non-contact force exists between them, and is it attractive or repulsive? Why?
(a) Protons, Neutrons, Electrons
Protons = atomic number = 12.
Neutrons = mass number − atomic number = 24 − 12 = 12.
Electrons = 12 (a neutral atom has equal numbers of protons and electrons so the +12 charge from protons cancels with the −12 charge from electrons).
(b) Location on Periodic Table
Group 2 (alkaline earth metals) and Period 3. Mg sits right below beryllium (Be) and right above calcium (Ca).
(c) Two Properties of Metals (any 2)
Shiny / lustrous when polished.
Good conductors of heat and electricity.
Malleable (can be hammered into sheets).
Ductile (can be drawn into wires).
Solid at room temperature (except mercury).
(d) Force Between Two Mg²⁺ Ions
Both ions have a charge of +2. According to the rule "like charges repel," the non-contact force between them is the electric force (electrostatic force), and it is repulsive — the ions push away from each other.

Reasoning: Two positive charges have the same sign, so they repel. (If one were +2 and the other −2, the force would be attractive instead.)
