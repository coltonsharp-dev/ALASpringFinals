Essay — Rubric & Exemplars
1 ESSAY · 25 PTS

Students chose ONE of two prompts. Sample claim and evidence pool shown for each. Rubric scores out of 25 across four dimensions.

OPTION 1 · WWI
“Was World War I inevitable, or could it have been avoided?”
SAMPLE CLAIM (either direction earns full credit if defended)
WWI was not strictly inevitable, but the MAIN conditions made it overwhelmingly likely — once those four pressures were in place, almost any sufficient spark would have ignited a continental war.
EVIDENCE POOL STUDENTS SHOULD DRAW FROM
  • The four MAIN causes (Militarism, Alliances, Imperialism, Nationalism) — pre-existing structural tensions
  • Pre-war arms race (Anglo-German naval rivalry, mass conscription)
  • The alliance chain reaction that pulled 6+ nations into war within weeks
  • Assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand, June 28, 1914 in Sarajevo by Gavrilo Princip
  • Industrialization fueling both competition and weapons production
  • Nationalism inside multi-ethnic empires (Austria-Hungary, Ottoman Empire)
  • Counterfactual angle: had alliances been weaker or militarism lower, the assassination might have stayed regional
OPTION 2 · INTERWAR
“How does a democracy collapse?”
SAMPLE CLAIM
A democracy collapses when economic crisis destroys public trust in democratic institutions at the same moment a demagogue successfully channels desperation into political extremism — and then uses the democracy's own legal structures to dismantle it from within.
EVIDENCE POOL STUDENTS SHOULD DRAW FROM
  • Weimar Republic born in defeat in 1919, blamed for the Treaty of Versailles
  • Hyperinflation 1923 — 4.2 trillion marks per U.S. dollar — destroyed savings
  • Great Depression triggered Oct 1929 — U.S. banks recalled loans; German factories collapsed
  • ~6 million unemployed (~33%) by 1932 — mass desperation
  • Nazi Party surge from 12 to 107 parliament seats (1928→1930) — economic crisis → extremism
  • Hitler legally appointed Chancellor January 1933 by Hindenburg/conservatives
  • Reichstag Fire February 1933 → emergency decrees suspending civil liberties
  • Enabling Act March 1933 — parliament voted to give Hitler decree power
  • Mid-1934 consolidation: parties banned, unions abolished, press controlled, offices merged after Hindenburg's death
  • Mussolini's 1922 March on Rome served as the proof-of-concept model
25 / A+
Exceptional. Clear, sophisticated claim that directly answers the prompt. 4+ pieces of specific evidence (named events, dates, terms, people). Reasoning explicitly explains how each piece of evidence proves the claim. Strong introduction and conclusion. Smooth writing.
22 / A
Strong. Clear claim, 3 pieces of specific evidence, solid reasoning connecting evidence to claim, organized structure. Minor gaps in depth or transitions.
19 / B
Competent. Identifiable claim, 2–3 pieces of evidence (some specific, some general), reasoning present but inconsistently developed. Adequate structure.
15 / C
Developing. Claim is vague or implicit. 1–2 pieces of evidence, mostly general rather than specific. Reasoning is thin or missing in places. Structure is loose.
10 / D
Weak. Major gaps: no clear claim, no specific evidence, or no reasoning. Or significant factual errors. The essay touches the prompt but does not actually argue.
5 / F
Insufficient. Off-topic, severely incomplete, or fundamentally inaccurate. Effort visible but the response does not meet basic requirements of an argument essay.
0
Blank, illegible, or completely off-prompt.
